Search the Database

Search 23,793 presentations from the SSWR Annual Conference (2005–2026) by topic, researcher, or institution.

How does this search work?

This is not generative AI. The search system does not create, summarize, or generate any text. Every result is a real presentation from the SSWR conference archives, shown exactly as it was submitted.

Keyword search finds presentations containing your exact words in the title or abstract — just like searching a document.

Semantic search goes further. Your query is converted into a mathematical representation (think of it as a “fingerprint” for the meaning of your words). This fingerprint is compared against pre-computed fingerprints for all 23,793 abstracts. Results are ranked by how closely their meaning matches yours — even if the exact words differ. For example, searching “how communities recover after natural disasters” will surface papers about post-disaster resilience, even though those exact words are not in your query. The embedding methodology used for semantic search is described in Perron, Victor, & Qi (in press, JSSWR).

Hybrid search combines both approaches: keyword matching for precision and semantic matching for breadth, merged into a single ranked list.

Match scores: Semantic and hybrid results display a percentage (e.g., “63% match”). This is a cosine similarity score — it measures how closely the meaning of your query aligns with each abstract. Scores above 60% indicate a strong topical match. Scores between 40–60% indicate related concepts. Scores below 40% are looser associations. These scores reflect similarity of meaning, not a quality or relevance judgment — a 50% match on a broad query may be exactly what you need.

Tips for semantic and hybrid search: Be descriptive. Use phrases like you would describe a topic to a colleague. More detail usually produces better results. If you know the exact term you are looking for, keyword search may be more effective.

Data quality notice

This database was constructed using AI-assisted methods applied to unstructured conference program data. While extensive validation was performed (94% institution accuracy, 91% position accuracy, Cohen’s kappa = .83 for methodology), errors and inconsistencies exist.

Institutional names in particular may appear in multiple forms (e.g., “University of Michigan” vs. “University of Michigan School of Social Work”) because the original program data was not standardized — further normalization is in progress. Author names, affiliations, and methodology classifications may also contain inaccuracies.

All affiliation data reflects what was reported at the time of the conference, not current positions.

If you find an error, please report it here — corrections with evidence are especially valuable.
