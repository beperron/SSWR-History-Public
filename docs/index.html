SSWR Conference History Database

A comprehensive, structured database of presentation abstracts from the Society for Social Work and Research Annual Conference, spanning two decades of scholarship (2005–2026). v0.9.0-beta

Motivation

Why This Project

Scientific conferences occupy a distinctive position in the research lifecycle. They are rarely the endpoint of scholarly work but often its first public articulation — where emerging ideas, methods, and findings are shared before formal peer-reviewed publication. Conference presentations provide an important indicator of where a discipline is investing its intellectual labor, who is participating, and where scholars might be innovating methodologically.

The SSWR Annual Conference is the primary research-focused conference for social work in North America. Yet unlike fields where conference proceedings are formally published and indexed, social work conference presentations are disseminated exclusively through conference programs designed for event navigation rather than analysis. Abstracts are spread across thousands of individual web pages on the Confex platform — each with its own URL, its own formatting quirks, and no standardized structure. Authors, affiliations, and methodological approaches are embedded in free-text fields rather than analyzable metadata. The formatting itself changed across years, requiring year-specific parsing logic to extract consistent records.

This project transforms that informational archive into analyzable research infrastructure. Using AI-assisted methods, we extracted, structured, and classified two decades of conference data — creating a resource that supports systematic hypothesis testing about research priorities, methodological traditions, and how social work scholarship develops over time.

This is an independent research project, not affiliated with or endorsed by the Society for Social Work and Research.

Overview

About the Database

The dataset is organized into two relational tables: papers (23,793 presentations with titles, abstracts, and methodology classifications) and paper_authors (69,924 author records with names, affiliations, positions, institutions, and geographic information). Records are linked by a shared paper identifier, enabling analyses at the presentation, author, institutional, and national levels.

Growth

Conference Presentations by Year

Annual presentations grew from 423 in 2005 to 1,935 in 2026, a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 7.5%.

AI-Ready

An AI-Ready Data Release

This dataset was designed from the start with AI in mind. Rather than simply posting files and a codebook, we carefully considered how researchers will interact with data in 2026 and beyond — through AI assistants, not manual code. The result is an AI-ready release:

  • MCP connections — pre-configured for Claude Code, Cursor, and OpenCode. Clone the repository and the database connection is automatic. Set up in 2 minutes.
  • Modular skills — four skill files (codebook, query patterns, semantic search, citation) that teach AI assistants how to work with this specific database correctly.
  • Vector embeddings — pre-computed for all 23,793 presentations, enabling semantic similarity search across two decades of scholarship.
  • Platform-agnostic — works with cloud models (Claude, GPT) and local models (Ollama + Qwen 3.5). No single vendor lock-in.

Why AI-ready? The scholarly contribution is never the twenty lines of code that load a CSV. It’s the question you ask, the verification you perform, and the interpretation you provide. AI-ready means the data comes pre-configured so researchers can skip the plumbing and focus on what actually matters: thinking about the research.

Data Quality

Quality Assurance

The database was constructed through an AI-assisted pipeline with human review at each major stage. Quality assurance included validation of web-scraped records against conference program indices, manual review of parsed affiliation fields (94% accuracy for institutions, 91% for positions in a 200-record sample), and inter-rater reliability testing for methodology classifications (Cohen’s kappa = .83).

Reporting Errors: Despite extensive quality assurance, we recognize that some errors will exist in a dataset of this scale. We welcome community contributions to improve data accuracy. If you identify an error, please submit a report through our data error reporting form.
